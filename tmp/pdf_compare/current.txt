Abstract
Rolling-element bearings are relatively small parts, but the reliability of many rotating
machines depends on them. A minor surface defect can start quietly and then develop
into strong vibration, heat generation, loss of alignment, damage to nearby components,
and eventually an unplanned shutdown. For this reason, estimating a bearing's remaining
useful life is an important problem in prognostics and health management. This thesis
uses the NASA IMS run-to-failure bearing dataset to study bearing remaining useful
life prediction and compares a physics-informed neural network with three data-driven
baseline models. At the start of the project, the expectation was that adding physical
constraints would clearly improve performance over a purely data-driven model. The nal
local experiments told a more nuanced story. The proposed DeepXDE physics-informed
model performed best in one of the three cross-bearing experiments, but the LSTM
baseline gave the lowest average RMSE and highest averageR 2 across the full experiment
set. This does not make the physics-informed approach unimportant. It shows that weak
physical regularization, when applied to noisy run-to-failure vibration features, must be
designed and weighted carefully. A simple monotonicity and fault-frequency prior can
help in some transfer settings, but it may also restrict the network when the degradation
path of the test bearing diers from the training bearings. The nal pipeline reads the IMS
data locally from extracted folders, removes the dependency on Google Drive, extracts
time-domain and envelope spectral features, constructs normalized RUL targets, builds a
PCA-based health indicator, and evaluates four models: a feed-forward neural baseline,
the proposed DeepXDE physics-informed model, an LSTM sequence model, and a 1D
CNN sequence model. The experiments use three train-validation-test splits designed to
test cross-bearing and cross-dataset transfer. The best average RMSE was achieved by
the LSTM baseline at 0.162, followed by the data-only feed-forward baseline at 0.185,
the proposed physics-informed model at 0.223, and the CNN baseline at 0.235. The work
contributes a reproducible local project structure, a documented feature and evaluation
pipeline, and an honest analysis of where the investigated physics-informed formulation
helped and where it did not.
Keywords:remaining useful life, rolling-element bearing, prognostics and health man-
agement, physics-informed neural network, IMS bearing dataset, vibration analysis,
LSTM, CNN.

Chapter 1
Introduction
1.1 BACKGROUND AND MOTIV ATION
Rolling-element bearings are modest in size, but many rotating machines depend on them
to run smoothly. They support shafts, carry loads, and reduce friction in motors, pumps,
turbines, gearboxes, fans, compressors, and other industrial equipment. When a defect
appears on a raceway or rolling element, it usually does not stay as a small local problem.
Each pass over the defect can create impacts, increase vibration, generate heat, aect
alignment, damage nearby components, and eventually stop the machine. In practice,
the bearing itself may be inexpensive compared with the downtime and secondary damage
caused by its failure.
Maintenance decisions therefore involve a balance between risk and waste. Reactive
maintenance waits until a fault becomes serious, which can lead to sudden shutdowns.
Preventive maintenance reduces some of that risk by replacing parts at scheduled inter-
vals, but the schedule rarely matches the true condition of every bearing. Some bearings
may be removed while they are still usable, while others may deteriorate before the
planned replacement date. Condition-based maintenance is more practical because it
looks at measured machine condition before making a decision. Prognostics and health
management takes this further by asking not only whether damage exists, but how much
useful operating time may still remain [2, 3].
Predicting remaining useful life is challenging because bearing degradation does not follow
a clean linear path. For much of a run, the vibration signal may appear almost stable, and
then it can change quickly as failure approaches. The measured response also depends
on fault type, speed, load, sensor placement, noise, resonance, lubrication, and bearing
geometry [4, 5]. As a result, a model trained on one bearing may not transfer well to
another bearing, even when both were tested on the same rig. Benchmark run-to-failure
datasets such as the IMS bearing dataset are useful because they give researchers access
to the same raw measurements and allow methods to be compared under repeatable
conditions [1].
Deep learning methods are now widely used in bearing diagnosis and RUL prediction

because they can learn nonlinear patterns from vibration data. CNNs can capture lo-
cal patterns in data windows, LSTMs can follow changes over time, and feed-forward
networks can model relationships in engineered feature vectors [68]. These models can
perform well when the training and test data are similar. The limitation is that they
usually learn from the examples they are given, without any built-in understanding of
how degradation should behave. When run-to-failure data are limited, or when the test
bearing follows a dierent degradation path from the training bearings, a data-only model
may produce predictions that look reasonable numerically but are dicult to justify phys-
ically.
Physics-informed neural networks oer a way to include prior engineering knowledge in
the learning process. In their most established form, PINNs train a neural network while
penalizing violations of governing equations or physical constraints [9]. Bearing RUL
prediction does not usually provide a complete closed-form degradation equation, but it
does provide useful partial knowledge. Damage should generally increase with operating
time, RUL should generally decrease, and bearing geometry gives characteristic fault
frequencies associated with cage, inner-race, outer-race, and rolling-element defects. This
thesis studies whether such weak physical information can improve RUL prediction on
the IMS data.
1.2 PROBLEM STATEMENT
The problem studied in this thesis is the prediction of normalized remaining useful life
for selected bearings in the IMS run-to-failure dataset. The main question is whether a
physics-informed neural network using time and fault-frequency information can gener-
alize better across bearing runs than comparable data-driven neural baselines.
This question is practical as well as methodological. In a real machine, the future failure
path is unknown. A maintenance engineer may have historical data from one bearing, but
the next bearing may not degrade in the same way. A useful RUL model must therefore
do more than memorize one run. It must learn a relationship between measured condition
and remaining life that transfers, at least partly, across bearings.
1.3 OBJECTIVES OF THE STUDY
The objectives of this thesis are:
1. To extract degradation-relevant vibration features from selected IMS bearing runs
and construct normalized RUL targets with a PCA-based health indicator for anal-
ysis.

3. To compare the proposed physics-informed model with data-only, LSTM, and CNN

1.4 SCOPE
This study is limited to the IMS bearing data placed locally underdata/raw/1st_test,
data/raw/2nd_test, anddata/raw/3rd_test. Four bearing runs are used in the nal
experiment design:ds2_b1,ds1_b3,ds1_b4, andds3_b3. The work uses vibration

data.
The models predict normalized RUL from extracted features. The thesis does not claim
eld deployment readiness. The data come from a controlled test rig, and the operating
conditions are much simpler than those in many industrial machines. The physics used
in the PINN is also modest on purpose. The model does not try to describe full bearing
contact mechanics, lubrication behavior, crack initiation, or crack growth. Instead, it
uses two engineering hints: RUL should generally decrease as damage accumulates, and
energy near bearing fault frequencies should carry useful information.
1.5 IMPORTANCE OF THE STUDY
This study is useful because it keeps the full bearing-prognostics chain visible. It starts
from the local IMS vibration les, extracts features that have a clear vibration meaning,
builds a health indicator, trains several neural models, and then checks whether those
models can predict RUL for bearings that were not used during training. In that sense,
the work is not only a model comparison. It is also a reproducible record of how raw
vibration measurements were turned into RUL results.
The study is also important because the result is not forced into a simple success story for
the proposed PINN. The LSTM baseline performed better on average, while the physics-
informed model gave the best RMSE in one experiment. This is closer to real modeling
work than a one-sided claim of improvement. It shows that physics-informed learning
can be helpful in some transfer cases, but the chosen physical prior must match the
data well enough to be useful. Reporting this mixed result gives a more honest starting
point for improving the model than simply claiming that adding physics always improves
prediction.

1.6 LIMITATIONS
The main limitations are:
1. The IMS dataset is valuable, but it contains only a small number of run-to-failure
histories. Because of that, the experiment splits have limited evidence to work
with. The results should be read as ndings for the selected IMS bearing runs,
not as proof that the same model ranking will hold for every bearing dataset or
industrial machine.
2. The physics prior used in this thesis is deliberately simple. It does not describe the
full mechanics of rolling contact, lubrication, crack initiation, or crack growth. It
only nudges the model with monotonic RUL behavior and fault-frequency energy,
so it can help when those assumptions t the held-out bearing and hurt when they
do not.
3. The model uses hand-engineered features, not raw vibration waveforms.
4. The RUL labels are derived from elapsed time to the end of each run, which assumes
the last available le represents failure or near-failure.
5. The experiments are run on one local PC, so training time and numerical results
may vary on other machines.

dataset, feature extraction, preprocessing, health indicator construction, model architec-
tures, and evaluation metrics. Chapter 4 describes the local implementation, experiment
splits, software environment, and reproducibility setup. Chapter 5 presents the results
and discusses model behavior across the three experiments. Chapter 6 summarizes the
ndings, contributions, limitations, and future work.

Chapter 2
Literature Review
2.1 INTRODUCTION
This chapter reviews the research background for bearing RUL prediction with physics-
informed neural networks. The discussion follows the structure of the thesis proposal,
but it is updated to reect the completed experiments. It begins with rolling-element
bearing failure modes and vibration-based health monitoring, then reviews predictive
maintenance methods, deep learning for bearing prognosis, general PINN theory, and
recent physics-informed learning work in bearing condition monitoring. The chapter
closes by identifying the research gap addressed in this thesis.
2.2 LITERATURE SUR VEY
2.2.1 Rolling-Element Bearings: Fundamentals and Failure Modes
A rolling-element bearing contains an inner race, an outer race, rolling elements, and
a cage. The rolling elements transfer load through small contact regions, so repeated
stress cycles can initiate subsurface or surface-initiated fatigue. Defects may also arise
from lubrication failure, contamination, overload, misalignment, electrical pitting, poor
installation, or manufacturing variation. Once a defect begins, repeated rolling contact
can grow it into a spall or crack that changes the vibration response of the machine [1012].
The vibration signature changes with the part of the bearing that is damaged. An outer-
race defect produces repeated impacts at a xed damaged region, while an inner-race
defect moves with the shaft and is aected by the load zone. Defects on the rolling
elements or cage create their own modulation patterns. This is why bearing monitoring
often looks at characteristic frequencies calculated from bearing geometry and shaft speed.
These frequencies should not be treated as exact labels for a fault, because real signals
include noise, resonance, and operating eects. Still, they give useful places to look in
the spectrum and envelope spectrum when searching for fault-related energy [4, 13].
The thesis proposal argued that bearing prognostics should not treat vibration features
as random columns in a table. That idea remains central in the completed work. RMS

is related to vibration energy, kurtosis reects impulsive behavior, crest factor captures
peak-to-average behavior, and envelope energy connects the signal to repeated impacts.
These features are still simplied summaries of the raw vibration signal, but they are
summaries with engineering meaning. Keeping that meaning visible makes the model
results easier to interpret, especially when a model performs well on one held-out bearing
and poorly on another.
2.2.2 Traditional Predictive Maintenance Techniques for Bearings
Predictive maintenance is the practical setting for this work. Instead of waiting for a
bearing to fail or replacing it only because a xed service interval has passed, the goal
is to use measured condition data to support a better maintenance decision. Jardine
et al. [2] describe this broader machinery diagnostics and prognostics framework, and
Lei et al. [3] review the path from data acquisition to RUL prediction. For bearings,
that path usually starts with sensor measurements, then moves through preprocessing,
feature extraction, health indicator construction, fault diagnosis, and nally future-life
estimation.
Vibration remains one of the most widely used signals for bearing health monitoring
because bearing faults generate impacts and modulations in the mechanical response.
Time-domain features such as RMS and kurtosis summarize energy and impulsiveness.
Frequency-domain and envelope-domain features can reveal periodic impact patterns
linked to bearing kinematics. Similarity-based and health-indicator methods then convert
these features into a degradation measure or RUL estimate [14].
The IMS bearing dataset is one of the common public run-to-failure datasets used for this
type of work. It is associated with the wavelet-lter bearing prognostics study of Qiu et
al. [1]. The PRONOSTIA platform is another well-known accelerated bearing degradation
benchmark [15]. Public datasets are valuable because they let dierent researchers test
methods on the same raw measurements. At the same time, public datasets are small
compared with industrial variability, so the train-test split must be designed carefully.
Random snapshot splits can overstate performance by allowing information from the same
run-to-failure trajectory into both training and testing.
2.2.3 Deep Learning in Predictive Maintenance
Deep learning has become popular in bearing prognostics because it can t nonlinear
relationships between sensor measurements and degradation labels. A feed-forward neu-
ral network can work directly with engineered feature vectors. A CNN can detect local

--- PAGE 19 ---
patterns from a sequence or transformed signal representation. An LSTM can model tem-
poral dependence and is therefore naturally suited to degradation trajectories. Babu et
al. [6] showed early CNN-based RUL regression, and later studies extended deep learning
for transfer learning, high-quality representation learning, and hybrid sequence architec-
tures [7, 8, 16].
Recent studies also combine convolutional, recurrent, and autoencoder structures for
bearing health monitoring and RUL prediction [1719]. These models are attractive be-
cause inference is fast after training and because they can learn patterns that are dicult
to specify manually. However, their performance depends strongly on the similarity be-
tween the training and test distributions. In bearing RUL prediction, this is a serious
issue because two bearings under similar nominal conditions may fail at dierent times
and with dierent feature trajectories.
For this reason, model evaluation should hold out complete bearing runs when the re-
search question is transfer across bearings. A random split of individual snapshots may
measure interpolation within one degradation curve rather than generalization to another
bearing. The experiment design in this thesis uses complete held-out runs for validation
and testing.
2.2.4 Physics-Informed Neural Networks
Physics-informed neural networks were introduced as a way to train neural networks
under physical residual constraints [9]. In the usual PINN setting, a neural network
approximates a eld variable, and automatic dierentiation is used to compute derivatives
that appear in a governing equation. The training loss combines data mismatch and a
physics residual. This has made PINNs attractive in uid mechanics, heat transfer, wave
propagation, and inverse problems where governing equations are available.
The PINN literature has grown quickly, and reviews by Lawal et al. [20] and Ren et
al. [21] show how broad the eld has become across scientic computing. However, a
model is not useful just because it combines a neural network with a physical equation.
The physical residual has to match the problem, carry enough useful information, and
be weighted so that it guides the model without overwhelming the data. It also has to
be trainable in practice. If the constraint is poorly chosen, the model may train more
slowly, t the data less accurately, or learn a biased solution.

is not governed by a single known partial dierential equation. Rolling contact fatigue,
lubrication, load distribution, resonance, material variation, and measurement noise in-

Paris and Erdogan [22] show how mechanics can describe damage propagation in some
contexts, but a complete bearing RUL equation for vibration features is rarely available in
practice. This motivates weaker physics-informed learning: monotonic damage behavior,
lifetime distributions, fault-frequency consistency, and other engineering priors.
2.2.5 Physics-Informed Neural Networks for Bearing Predictive Mainte-
nance
Recent bearing-related studies have begun to use physics-informed or knowledge-informed
learning. Chen et al. [23] proposed a physics-informed deep neural network for bearing
prognosis with multisensory signals. Parziale et al. [24] applied PINNs to condition
monitoring of rotating shafts. Herwig et al. [25] developed a signal-processing and physics-
informed network for explainable bearing condition monitoring. Zhong et al. [26] used a
multi-source physics-informed SincNet structure for rolling bearing fault diagnosis. von
Hahn and Mechefske [27] investigated a knowledge-informed loss based on Weibull lifetime
behavior.
These studies show why the approach is promising. Physics can improve interpretabil-
ity, guide learning when data are limited, and discourage predictions that conict with
engineering expectations. They also show why the approach must be tested carefully.
A physical prior only helps when it matches the measured data closely enough. If the
prior is too weak, the model may behave like a data-only network. If it is too strong or
mismatched, it may prevent the model from tting valid degradation patterns.
2.3 RESEARCH GAP
The literature shows three gaps that motivate this thesis. First, many bearing RUL
studies report strong results, but their preprocessing, RUL labeling, train-test split, and
evaluation protocol dier. This makes comparisons dicult. Second, some studies use
random sample splits that can leak run-specic degradation information into both training
and testing. Third, physics-informed learning for bearing prognosis is still developing,
and there is no guarantee that a weak physical prior improves prediction on complete
held-out bearing runs.
This thesis addresses a narrower and reproducible question: how does a weak physics-
informed RUL model behave when compared with common neural baselines on complete
held-out IMS bearing runs? The study uses a local project structure, explicit feature
extraction, a dened RUL target, saved tables and gures, and three train-validation-test
splits. The goal is not to claim a universal best model. The goal is to test a specic

3.4 FEATURE EXTRACTION
Each vibration snapshot is converted into a feature vector. The time-domain features are
RMS, standard deviation, peak-to-peak value, kurtosis, crest factor, and mean absolute
value. These features summarize amplitude, spread, impulsiveness, and peak behavior.
They are widely used in bearing condition monitoring because they are simple and phys-
ically interpretable.
The physics-related features are calculated from envelope spectral energy near bearing
fault frequencies. The signal is centered, transformed with the Hilbert transform to obtain
the envelope, centered again, windowed with a Hann window, and transformed with a
real FFT. For each fault frequency, the pipeline sums spectral energy in a 5 Hz band
around the rst four harmonics. The four energy features areE F T F,E BP F O,E BP F I, and
EBSF . Their sum is stored asE kin.

3.5 RUL TARGET LABELING
For each selected bearing run, elapsed time is computed from the timestamp of the rst
snapshot. RUL is computed by subtracting the current timestamp from the timestamp
of the nal snapshot. The model target is normalized RUL:
RU Lnorm(t) = RU L(t)
max(RU L).(3.5)
This gives a target near 1 at the beginning of the run and near 0 at the end. The
normalization makes results comparable across runs with dierent durations. It also
means that the model predicts relative life fraction, not absolute hours.
3.6 PREPROCESSING
Feature values can dier greatly between bearing runs. To reduce the eect of run-specic
scale, each run is normalized using its early healthy baseline. The rst 5 percent of sam-
ples, with a minimum of 20 samples, are treated as the healthy baseline. For each model
feature, the median baseline value is used. The feature is converted to a nonnegative
relative increase, transformed withlog1p, and smoothed with a rolling median of 15
samples.
Elapsed time is converted toelapsed_scaledby dividing elapsed hours by 1200.
The model input vector containselapsed_scaled, the normalized feature set, and
sigma_H_norm. The nal variable is a constant placeholder kept for compatibility with

a monotonic degradation signal. The model feature matrix is standardized and projected
onto the rst principal component. The resulting component is scaled between 0 and
1. If the indicator direction is inverted, it is ipped so that higher values correspond to
greater damage.
The health indicator is not used as the nal RUL target. It is used as an explanatory

3.8 MODEL DEFINITIONS
Four models are evaluated. The data-only neural baseline is a feed-forward neural network
implemented in DeepXDE [28]. It uses three hidden layers with 64 neurons per layer and
16

--- PAGE 29 ---
tanh activation. A sigmoid output transform constrains predictions between 0 and 1. It
is trained with mean squared error.
The proposed physics-informed model uses the same basic feed-forward architecture but
adds weak physical residuals. The rst residual penalizes positive RUL slope with respect
to time beyond a small tolerance, because RUL should generally decrease as life is con-
sumed. The second residual connects highE kin to damage by encouraging the predicted
damage term1−RU Lto be consistent with fault-frequency energy. The model loss can
be written as
L=L data +λ mLmono +λ eLenergy ,(3.6)
whereL data is the supervised RUL loss,L mono is the monotonicity residual,L energy is the
fault-energy residual, andλ m andλ e are small regularization weights.
The LSTM baseline uses sliding windows of feature vectors. It reads a sequence of length
20 and predicts the RUL at the end of the sequence. The architecture has one LSTM
layer with 64 hidden units followed by a small dense head and a sigmoid output. The
CNN baseline uses the same sequence windows but applies 1D convolutions across the
time axis.

3.10 EV ALUATION METRICS
The models are evaluated with MAE, RMSE, andR 2. MAE gives the average size of the
prediction error, so it is the easiest metric to read as a typical RUL mistake. RMSE looks
at the same errors but gives more weight to large misses. This matters in maintenance
planning because a few large RUL errors can be more damaging than many small ones.
R2 gives a broader check on whether the model is actually following the target trend.
WhenR 2 is negative, the model is doing worse than a very simple prediction that always
uses the mean target value.
RMSE is used as the main ranking metric in this thesis because the study is concerned
with avoiding large RUL errors on held-out bearings. MAE is reported alongside it to
show the average error level, andR 2 is included to show how well each model follows the
overall RUL variation. Using all three metrics gives a more balanced view than relying on
a single score. The metric denitions follow common prognostics evaluation practice [29].

5.6 DISCUSSION OF THE PHYSICS-INFORMED MODEL
The proposed PINN was built around two simple engineering ideas. The rst idea is that
RUL should generally move downward as operating time is consumed. The second idea
is that energy near bearing fault-frequency harmonics should contain useful information
about damage. Both ideas are reasonable, but they are still only partial descriptions of
how a real bearing fails.
The monotonicity residual is correct in principle because the true remaining life of a
bearing should not increase as the test continues. The diculty is that the model does
not see the true damage process directly. It sees vibration features that can be noisy,
delayed, or irregular. If a held-out bearing develops a dierent feature pattern from the
training bearings, the monotonicity term can pull the prediction toward a smoother curve
than the data support. The implementation uses a tolerance and a small weight to reduce
this problem, but the eect still depends on the train-test split.
The spectral prior has the same limitation. Higher combined fault-frequency energy is a
sensible sign of bearing degradation, but it is not the same thing as damage. Envelope
energy can change because of resonance, noise, sensor direction, load-zone eects, and
the stage at which a fault becomes visible in the signal. IfE kin rises late in one bearing
32

--- PAGE 45 ---
run but earlier in another, a model trained on one pattern may apply the prior at the
wrong part of the life cycle.
The main lesson is that physics-informed learning is not automatically better just because
engineering terms are included in the loss function. The physical prior has to match the
measured data closely enough to guide the model in the right direction. In this thesis, the
prior helped in Experiment 2, but it was not strong or complete enough to outperform
sequence modeling overall.
5.7 WHY THE LSTM PERFORMED WELL
The LSTM performed well mainly because it used recent history instead of judging each
snapshot by itself. Bearing degradation is a process, so the meaning of one feature value
depends on what happened before it. A moderate RMS value, for example, could belong
to an early healthy stage in one run or to a temporary plateau after fault growth in
another. A short sequence gives the model more context for making that distinction.
The LSTM also does not require a xed assumption about the exact relationship be-
tweenE kin and damage. Instead, it learns from the shape and timing of the feature
sequence. This exibility is likely why it achieved the best average RMSE andR 2 across
the completed experiments.
5.8 WHY THE CNN WON EXPERIMENT 3
The CNN result is more split-specic. It had the weakest average RMSE, but it was the
best model in Experiment 3. This shows why the individual experiment results matter
and not only the average table. A 1D CNN can detect local patterns inside a sliding
feature window. In Experiment 3, those local patterns transferred well to the held-out
ds1_b3run. In the other experiments, the same local-pattern approach did not transfer
as strongly.
This does not make the CNN the best overall model. It means that dierent held-out
bearings can favor dierent model behaviors. For this reason, the split-wise analysis is
important. A single average score is useful, but it can hide cases where a model succeeds
for one bearing and fails for another.
5.9 PRACTICAL IMPLICATIONS
For maintenance use, the safest conclusion is cautious. The current PINN formulation
should not be treated as a ready eld predictor. The LSTM baseline was stronger in the
completed experiments, but it also should not be deployed without more validation. A
33

--- PAGE 46 ---
practical RUL system would need more run-to-failure data, tests under multiple operating
conditions, uncertainty estimates, and a clear rule for how a prediction is converted into
a maintenance decision.
The results still give a useful direction. Physics-informed features remain valuable because
they help connect the model output back to vibration behavior. The PINN framework
is also still worth developing, but the physical part of the model needs to be more in-
formative than the weak priors used here. The next step is to improve the degradation
constraint, test stronger physics-based losses, and evaluate the models on more bearing
runs and datasets.

Chapter 6
Conclusion
6.1 SUMMARY
This thesis studied remaining useful life prediction for rolling-element bearings using the
IMS run-to-failure dataset. The work started from a notebook, but the nal version was
converted into a local Python project so the experiment could be run and checked more
reliably. The completed pipeline reads the raw IMS folders, extracts time-domain and
envelope spectral features, builds normalized RUL labels, creates a PCA-based health
indicator, trains the neural models, and saves the nal tables and gures.
The proposed model was a DeepXDE physics-informed neural network. Its physical part
was intentionally simple: it encouraged RUL to decrease with operating time and used
fault-frequency energy as a weak indication of damage. The model was compared with
three baselines: a data-only feed-forward network, an LSTM sequence model, and a
CNN sequence model. The experiments used complete held-out bearing runs so that the
comparison tested transfer across bearings rather than only interpolation within the same
run.
The results were mixed, which is an important outcome of the study. The proposed
PINN achieved the best RMSE in Experiment 2, but it did not perform best overall. The
LSTM baseline gave the strongest average result, with average RMSE 0.162 and average
R2 0.646. The data-only baseline was second by average RMSE. The proposed PINN
ranked third, and the CNN ranked fourth on average, although the CNN was the best
model in Experiment 3. These results show that adding a weak physical prior can help in
some cases, but it does not guarantee better RUL prediction across all held-out bearings.
6.2 CONTRIBUTIONS
The main contributions of this thesis are:
1. A complete local workow for IMS bearing RUL prediction, converted from the
original notebook into a project that can be rerun from the command line.
2. A documented feature extraction process that combines common time-domain vi-
35

--- PAGE 48 ---
bration features with envelope spectral energy near bearing fault frequencies.
3. A PCA-based health indicator analysis used to check whether the extracted features
show a meaningful degradation trend.
4. A DeepXDE physics-informed RUL model that adds monotonicity and fault-
frequency energy priors to a supervised neural model.
5. A comparison against feed-forward, LSTM, and CNN baselines using complete held-
out bearing splits, which gives a stricter test of cross-bearing generalization.
6. An honest interpretation of the nal evidence, including the cases where the pro-
posed PINN helped and the cases where it did not outperform the baselines.
6.3 LIMITATIONS
The study has several limitations. The IMS data provide only a small number of complete
failure runs. The RUL target is normalized by the nal timestamp, so it does not represent
an independently measured failure threshold. The physical prior is weak and does not
include a full contact fatigue model, lubrication model, temperature eect, or uncertainty
estimate. The model is trained on engineered features rather than raw waveforms. Finally,
the results are from one local implementation and one set of hyperparameters.
6.4 FUTURE WORK
Future work should test stronger physics-informed formulations. Possible directions in-
clude a learned degradation state constrained by monotonic damage growth, uncertainty-
aware RUL prediction, Weibull or crack-growth inspired lifetime priors, and multi-task
learning that predicts both health indicator and RUL. The experiments should also be
repeated on PRONOSTIA and other bearing datasets so that conclusions do not depend
on one benchmark.
The feature pipeline can also be improved. Time-frequency representations, adaptive
envelope bands, bearing-specic resonance selection, and raw waveform sequence models
may capture information lost by the current feature set. More careful hyperparameter
tuning and repeated random seeds would make the comparison more statistically reliable.
6.5 FINAL CONCLUSION
The completed experiments show that physics-informed bearing RUL prediction is
promising but not automatic. In this thesis, the proposed PINN improved the result
in one experiment but did not beat the LSTM baseline overall. The best model was the

one that used temporal context most eectively. A practical bearing prognostics system
should therefore combine physical interpretation with sequence-aware learning, and it
should be tested under complete run-level splits before strong claims are made.
